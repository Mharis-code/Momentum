# Case Study — Turn a Client Result into Proof

## What This Skill Does

Most solopreneurs get client results and let them disappear. The client says "thanks, this is great" and the work goes undocumented. Case Study captures that result, structures it into a repeatable proof asset, and turns it into four usable pieces: a one-pager for the website, a LinkedIn story post, a DM proof piece for sales conversations, and a referral ask that converts the happy client into a source of new leads.

One good result, used correctly, is worth months of content.

**What you get:**
- Pre-interview question set — specific questions to ask the client to extract quantifiable proof
- Before/after case study (one-pager) — the structured story with numbers
- LinkedIn story post — 200–350 words, first-person or third-person, narrative-led
- DM proof piece — 3–5 sentences to drop into a sales conversation as social proof
- Referral ask template — one message that turns the documented result into a referral opportunity

**Saved to:** `projects/case-studies/[client-slug]/`

---

## Embedded Knowledge — Why Proof Compounds

From Hormozi ($100M Leads): others advertising for you is the most efficient form of lead generation. A closed client who produces a documented result and refers one person has generated more leverage than any ad campaign for a solo operator.

The proof flywheel:
1. Client gets a result
2. You document it with their permission
3. You share it as content
4. Content produces warm leads who already trust you based on the result
5. Warm leads convert faster because the trust is pre-built
6. Faster conversions produce more results
7. Cycle repeats

The timing matters. The best moment to ask for proof is immediately after the first visible result — not at the end of the engagement, not after the final invoice is paid. The emotional peak is the first time something clicks. That is when the quote is authentic, specific, and compelling.

**The specific question method:** Do not ask "write me a testimonial." That produces generic, unusable copy. Ask one specific question: "When [specific thing you set up] started working, what was the first moment you realized it was worth doing?" The specificity triggers a real memory. The real memory produces real language. Real language is what sells.

## Step 2: Generate Pre-Interview Questions

If the result is described but lacks specific proof (no numbers, no before/after contrast, no emotional moment), generate a set of pre-interview questions to send to or ask the client.

Write 6 questions in this order:

**Q1 — The Before:**
"Before we worked together, what was your biggest frustration or bottleneck with [the problem area]? What did a typical [day / week / month] look like?"

**Q2 — The Decision:**
"What made you decide to do something about it? Was there a specific moment or event that triggered it?"

**Q3 — The Turning Point:**
"When [specific thing we built] started working, what was the moment you realized it was worth it? What did you notice first?"

**Q4 — The Numbers:**
"What measurable change have you seen? Even rough estimates help — hours saved per week, cost reduction, revenue added, errors prevented."

**Q5 — The Unexpected:**
"Was there anything that surprised you — something you didn't expect to change but did?"

**Q6 — The Recommendation:**
"Would you recommend this to someone in a similar situation? What would you tell them?"

Tell the user: "Send these questions to [client name] before the testimonial call, or ask them live and take notes. Bring the answers back to me and I'll write the full case study."

## Step 3: Write the Before/After Case Study (One-Pager)

Structure:

```
# Case Study: [One-sentence result headline]

## Client
[Role/title] in [niche/industry] — [anonymized or with permission: first name + company type]

## The Situation Before
[2–3 sentences: what they were dealing with, in their language if available]
Key pain: [specific frustration, quoted if possible]

## What We Built / What Changed
[2–3 sentences: the specific thing delivered — not "we implemented AI" but "we built a 3-step intake automation that routes new client requests to the right team member and sends a same-day response"]

## The Result
[Numbers first, then context]
- [Metric 1]: [before] → [after]
- [Metric 2]: [before] → [after]
- Time to result: [how long before they saw this change]

## In Their Words
"[Direct quote — specific, emotional, real. If not available, mark as: QUOTE NEEDED]"

## What This Means for Similar Businesses
[1–2 sentences: generalize the result to make it relevant to the reader who is like this client]
```

Rules:
- Use their exact words wherever possible — mark as "their words" or quote directly
- If a number is estimated, state it as estimated: "saved approximately 8 hours/week (client's estimate)"
- Never exaggerate results. A modest real result beats an impressive fabricated one.
- If no quote is available, leave the quote section blank and flag it for follow-up

## Step 4: Write the LinkedIn Story Post

Length: 200–350 words.

Structure:
1. **Opening line (hook):** A specific number or a before/after contrast. Never "I helped a client recently." Start with the result or the tension.
2. **The before:** 2–3 sentences establishing who they were and what they were struggling with.
3. **The turning point:** What changed. Be specific. What did they build, set up, or realize?
4. **The result:** Numbers, then narrative context.
5. **The lesson:** One sentence extracting the transferable insight for the reader.
6. **CTA:** Soft — "If you're dealing with something similar, DM me [keyword]" or "I work with [avatar type] on exactly this — details in my profile."

Tone: first-person narrator (you telling the story) OR third-person story (they did X). Do not switch mid-post.
No emojis. No em dashes. One idea per paragraph.

---

## Step 5: Write the DM Proof Piece

A 3–5 sentence block designed to drop into a sales DM conversation when a prospect asks "do you have any examples?" or when you want to establish credibility mid-conversation.

Format:
"[Client type] came to me [with the problem]. [What we built in one sentence]. [The result in numbers or specific outcome]. [Optional: a one-sentence quote from the client]. Happy to share more if relevant to what you're working on."

Write 2 variants:
- **Variant A:** For when the result is strong and the numbers are clear
- **Variant B:** For when the result is more qualitative or early-stage

---

## Step 6: Write the Referral Ask Template

Send this to the client after documenting their result. Timing: immediately after the case study is drafted and shared with them for approval.

"Hey [Name], I just wrote up the work we did together — [one-line result summary]. I'm going to use it as a case study to show other [avatar type] what's possible.

Quick favor: do you know one or two other [avatar type] who are dealing with [the problem]? If so, the easiest thing is a quick intro over email — even just 'Hey, you should talk to [your name], they helped me [result in one line]' is enough.

No pressure at all — just thought I'd ask while the results are fresh. And thank you again for letting me document this."

## Hard Rules

- Never fabricate numbers. If the result was "they felt more organized," write that — don't convert it to a time saving estimate without their confirmation.
- The before state must be specific. "They were struggling with efficiency" is not a before state. "They were spending 4 hours every Monday manually compiling reports from 3 different tools" is.
- The quote must be real or marked as QUOTE NEEDED. Do not invent quotes.
- The LinkedIn post must start with the result or the tension — not with "I" or "My client" or "Recently."
- Ask for client approval before publishing anything with identifying details. Mark the case study with "[APPROVAL NEEDED]" until confirmed.
